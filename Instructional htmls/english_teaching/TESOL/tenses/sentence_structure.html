There are two types of sentence structures; Based on Complexity and Conditional Sentences

Simple vs Complex Structures of Sentences

Here there are main for types of sentences: 1. Simple Sentences, 2. Compound Sentences, 3. Complex Sentences, and 4. Compound-complex Sentences.

1. Simple Sentences

  • These are made by one independent clause, and nothing else.
  • They can be in one of any of the following formats:

    • Subject + verb to be + nouns

    He is a teacher.

    She is a dentist.

    Subject + verb to be + adjectives

    He is slow.

    They are efficient.

    Subject + verb to be + adjectives + nouns

    She is a tall woman.

    They are a loud group.

    Subject + Verb (+ adjectives, adverbs + adjectives; and so on.)

    He slept.

    She snores.

  • Make sure they accumulate enough vocabulary about different types of words (nouns and adjectives especially) before moving on from this stage.

    • Teach each of the above forms of the simple sentence structure with a new vocabulary everyday, grouped into more manageable groups like kitchen ware, types of house furniture, different types of jobs and job titles, and so on.

Compound Sentences

  • These are made by a combination of two independent clauses.

    • Eg:

    She is a fast learner and he is a good teacher.

    They are good swimmers but they can't drive.
  • Students should have a fairly good understanding of coordinating conjunctions before they are introduced to this type of sentences.

Complex Sentences

  • These are sentences with one independent clause and at least one dependent clause.

    • She hardly speaks when she is angry.

    Once she comes, show her where the kitchen is.

  • The rule is: When the dependent clause comes before the independent one, they have to be separated by a comma. When the independent clause comes first, there is no comma between them.
  • Students should really know subordinate conjuctions for them to be able to form these sentences. You can teach these two subjects together. Start by teaching them what are dependent clauses, then show them how subordinate conjuctions come into play.

Compound-complex sentences

  • These are formed by the combination of two independent clauses and at least one dependent clauses.
  • These are sentences used in advanced level English, so teach them only when you think they've enough vocabulary and grammar to understand you.

    • Eg.

    She runs, but when she walks, she walks slow.
