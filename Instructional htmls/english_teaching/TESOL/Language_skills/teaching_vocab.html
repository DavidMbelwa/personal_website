Meaning, Form, and Pronounciation

  • These are things you focus on when you're teaching new vocabulary.
  • Meaning is the first thing you teach, so there isn't much fuss there.
  • Form is the type of the word; whether it is a noun, a verb, etc. It is crucial that your students know to differentiate different types / forms of words early on since it will help them know which words to use where.
  • Flash cards are your best options for both meaning and form, but try to introduce some creativity in teaching form so that your students are comfortable with the backbone of things. You can introduce word streets to young children, where each apartment is a collection of a single type of words and students are required to add tenants into them based on groups. Eg, vocabulary about family and kitchen ware resides in the "Noun" apartment, concrete and abstract verb in their own apartment, etc.
  • For Pronounciation, the huge part of the job is on you the teacher to know special sounds in English and how they're pronounced. There're things we'll look at later in the speaking section, but you need to know that you'll have to teach your students how to pronounce watches for example, as different as leaves is pronounced.
  • Understanding a standard model of pronounciation for suffixes will help a lot. It is also a standard platform that your students will learn to base their pronounciations on.
  • For teaching pronounciation, use games, role playing and reading aloud. Also, make your own pronounciations as clear and as consistent as you can, because your students will largely learn from how you spell things.

Collocations

  • These are words that are used together in the everyday lives of native speakers.

    • Eg

    - Give me a minute
    - Do your best.
    - Heavy traffic, and so on..
  • The only trick for mastering collocations is understanding the context of where/when they're used. So teach them in that a way.
  • Collocations are not as important as other grammar points, and students will invent their own combination of words to describe a similar situation. Most likely, a native would understand them. So just do your best to teach them the right alternatives when you hear them do that. Give them a list of common collocations and tell them to find their meaning. That'll also probably help. Or if you make it a class one day to teach different collocations and how they're used.
  • The type of students also determines how much effort you should put on collocations. Students who are learning for academic purposes may not need collocations that much compared to someone who is training in order to be able to interact with native speakers in real-life situations.
